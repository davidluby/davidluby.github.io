Fourier Transform

Any arbitrary signal can be replicated by sums, or collections, of constituent signals. The figures below demonstrate this phenomena in replicating the function y(t) = 3t exactly using unique sums of constituent signals.

The same phenomena can be observed using sums of sinusoids to replicate signals. Most basically, 2sin(t) can be characterized by and is exactly equal to sin(t) + sin(t) or 4sin(t) - 2sin(t). The former and latter are reffered to as constructive and destructive interference, where the former results in an increase in amplitude and a reduction in the latter.

Each summation of constituent signals used thus far resulted in a perfect replication of the desired arbitrary signal, but perfection is replaced by approximation in practice. Below are four approximations of a piecewise, triangular signal using n sinusoids. Notice that as n increases, or approaches infinity, the accuracy of the approximatation, or replication, increases.

Each constituent sinusoid interferes--constructively or destructively--with each of the others. The result as the number, n, of constituent signals increases is increased accuracy of approximation. But, what if there was a way to do this backwards, such that the replicated signal becomes the input, and a sum of constituent signals are the new output? Enter the Fourier transform--a mathematical operation that converts an arbitrary signal into a sum of sinusoids.
